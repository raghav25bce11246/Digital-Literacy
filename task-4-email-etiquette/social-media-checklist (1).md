# Social Media Do's and Don'ts for College Students
**Task 4 – Email Etiquette | Part B**
*Prepared: March 29, 2026*

---

## ✅ DO's — Responsible Social Media Practices

| # | Do This | Why It Matters |
|---|---------|----------------|
| 1 | **Think before you post** — ask yourself if you'd be comfortable with a future employer, professor, or family member seeing it. | Your digital footprint is permanent. Even deleted posts can be screenshotted and shared. |
| 2 | **Keep your profiles professional** — use a clear photo, accurate bio, and consistent, respectful language across platforms. | Recruiters and professors often review social profiles; a professional presence builds credibility. |
| 3 | **Use privacy settings wisely** — review and update who can see your posts, personal details, and tagged content. | Controlling your audience reduces the risk of unwanted attention, data misuse, or identity theft. |
| 4 | **Give credit where it's due** — tag original creators and cite sources when sharing articles, images, or ideas. | Sharing uncredited content is a form of plagiarism and can damage your academic and professional reputation. |
| 5 | **Engage positively and constructively** — support peers, share useful resources, and participate in respectful discussions. | Positive engagement builds your network and reflects well on your character both online and offline. |
| 6 | **Report harmful or abusive content** — use platform tools to flag misinformation, harassment, or inappropriate material. | Being an active, responsible digital citizen helps keep online spaces safe for everyone. |
| 7 | **Fact-check before sharing** — verify information from a credible source before reposting news or claims. | Spreading misinformation — even unintentionally — can harm others and erode trust in you as a source. |

---

## ❌ DON'Ts — Habits to Avoid

| # | Avoid This | Why It's Harmful |
|---|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Don't share personal information publicly** — avoid posting your address, phone number, daily schedule, or financial details. | Oversharing makes you vulnerable to stalking, scams, and identity theft. |
| 2 | **Don't post in anger or frustration** — avoid venting about professors, classmates, or institutions in a public forum. | Emotional posts are hard to retract and can damage relationships, academic standing, or future job prospects. |
| 3 | **Don't engage in or tolerate cyberbullying** — do not send, share, or react positively to content that mocks or harasses individuals. | Cyberbullying causes serious mental health harm and may carry legal consequences on campus. |
| 4 | **Don't plagiarise or misrepresent yourself** — do not claim others' work, fake credentials, or misrepresent your academic record online. | Academic dishonesty extends to social media and can result in disciplinary action or loss of opportunities. |
| 5 | **Don't ignore the long-term impact of photos and videos** — avoid posting images from parties, protests, or situations that could be taken out of context. | Visual content spreads rapidly and can define your reputation long after the moment has passed. |
| 6 | **Don't overshare real-time location** — avoid live-posting your location, especially when alone or in an unfamiliar place. | Broadcasting your whereabouts in real time poses personal safety risks. |
| 7 | **Don't use social media as a substitute for direct communication** — avoid addressing serious conflicts, complaints, or grievances on a public platform. | Public callouts escalate situations unnecessarily; direct and private communication is always more effective and respectful. |
